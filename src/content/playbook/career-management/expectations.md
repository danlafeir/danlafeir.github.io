The framework is built around one core principle: [know your one job and do it first](https://charity.wtf/2021/03/07/know-your-one-job-and-do-it-first/). Master your current level before reaching for the next. Scope of impact grows with each level — from yourself, to your pair, to your team, to the organization, and beyond.

## L1 — Curious IC

You're building your foundation. The primary signals are **aptitude and attitude** — are you learning, asking good questions, and validating your assumptions with peers? At this level, it's more important to be curious and coachable than to be right.

- **Engineering competency:** You're learning the fundamentals — your team's tech stack, version control, and testing practices; you ask questions before making assumptions and validate your understanding with peers.
- **Influence:** Your scope is yourself; you earn trust through reliability — doing what you said you'd do.
- **Mentorship/coaching:** You seek out feedback actively and act on it; you're the one being coached.
- **Impact:** Your work is scoped to individual tasks; you complete them reliably and surface blockers early rather than going silent.
- **Communication:** You ask good questions, check your understanding before proceeding, and keep your pair updated on where you are.

## L2 — Strong IC

You deliver quality work independently. The team can hand you a problem and trust you to see it through.

- **Engineering competency:** You write clean, well-tested code without supervision; you practice TDD and hold yourself to it; you rotate pairs without missing a beat.
- **Influence:** Your influence is your own delivery; teammates trust you to own work end-to-end without hand-holding.
- **Mentorship/coaching:** You give written feedback to peers — specific, actionable, and timely; you're beginning to help newer engineers find their footing.
- **Impact:** You deliver reliably and are aligned with the team mission; you can articulate why the work matters, not just what it does.
- **Communication:** You communicate clearly about blockers and keep the team updated without being asked; you can explain your technical decisions to teammates.

## L3 — Strong IC with Team Impact

Your influence extends beyond your own delivery. You're shaping how the team works.

- **Engineering competency:** You refactor code to apply technical principles at scale; you identify architectural problems before they become production incidents.
- **Influence:** You shape how the team approaches problems; teammates adopt practices you've introduced because they work.
- **Mentorship/coaching:** You educate teammates on technical principles through code review and pairing; newer engineers on your team improve because of your engagement.
- **Impact:** Your self-directed improvements benefit the whole team; you balance assigned work with proactive investments that raise the team's floor.
- **Communication:** You facilitate meetings that produce clear, actionable outcomes; you can explain technical tradeoffs to the team without needing to defer to others.

## L4 — Exemplary IC amplifying the team

Your scope is the organization. You operate in ambiguous, high-stakes areas and make others better.

- **Engineering competency:** You own solution architecture end-to-end; you're the person teams call for genuinely hard technical problems; your architectural decisions account for the broader business context, not just the technical tradeoffs.
- **Influence:** You operate across the organization; your presence on a problem raises the quality of the outcome even when you're not the one writing the code.
- **Mentorship/coaching:** You mentor team members intentionally; you scale your knowledge through documentation and tooling, not just conversations.
- **Impact:** Your work removes organizational bottlenecks; you drive iterative delivery on complex, multi-stakeholder problems.
- **Communication:** You articulate architectural changes within the broader business context and earn stakeholder buy-in — not just team approval.

## L5 — Technical leader on the team, influencing peer teams

Your scope reaches beyond your team. You're the person peer teams seek out before making technical decisions that touch yours.

- **Engineering competency:** Your code and reviews set the quality ceiling for your team; you design solutions that work cleanly across team boundaries and flag where system seams create risk before they become incidents.
- **Influence:** Adjacent teams factor your technical opinions into their decisions; you're consulted, not just informed.
- **Mentorship/coaching:** You're actively developing 1–2 engineers toward L4; you can describe exactly what each one needs and why.
- **Impact:** You connect your team's work to the broader product roadmap; your changes improve systems your team doesn't own — engineers you've never worked with encounter better code because of decisions you made.
- **Communication:** You write design docs that adjacent teams actually use; you can represent technical tradeoffs clearly to non-engineers on your own team.

## L6 — Technical leader on the team, amplifying peer teams

You don't just influence how peer teams work — you actively make them better. Your patterns and practices spread because they're genuinely useful.

- **Engineering competency:** You identify and remove systemic quality problems that no single team owned; your architectural patterns are adopted across teams; you've simplified something significant, not just added to it.
- **Influence:** Peer teams change how they work based on your input; your voice carries weight in cross-team technical decisions without requiring a formal role.
- **Mentorship/coaching:** You develop engineers across teams, not just your own; you've helped someone reach L4 or materially accelerated a peer team engineer's growth.
- **Impact:** Your changes measurably reduce friction for multiple teams; you translate multi-team priorities into a coherent technical roadmap and anticipate cross-team conflicts before they surface as blockers.
- **Communication:** You write documents that change how teams think, not just what they do; you communicate proactively upward about cross-team technical health.

## L7 — Technical leader for a domain, influencing the system architecture

Your scope is a technical domain. You understand the full shape of the system and are shaping its next generation of architectural decisions.

- **Engineering competency:** Your reviews improve engineers' mental models, not just the PR; you can articulate where the system is healthy and where it's fragile across team boundaries; you're shaping the next major architectural decisions.
- **Influence:** Engineering leads across the org bring you into their hardest technical discussions; your judgment on system design carries weight with senior leadership.
- **Mentorship/coaching:** You're explicitly developing engineers toward L5–L6; you have a clear point of view on each person's path and what they need to get there.
- **Impact:** You hold a 12–18 month technical vision for your domain connected to company priorities; your decisions set constraints for other teams — a wrong call at your level takes multiple quarters to unwind.
- **Communication:** You make the case for major technical investments to non-technical stakeholders; your written thinking shapes org-wide decisions.

## L8 — Technical leader for a domain, amplifying the system architecture

You don't just influence the system architecture — you've moved it. You've driven significant change and made it stick.

- **Engineering competency:** The quality bar you set is the quality bar for your domain; you've navigated the org dynamics required to drive architectural change across teams and made it durable.
- **Influence:** Other technical leaders defer to your judgment on the hardest system-wide decisions; you shape technical culture, not just technical decisions.
- **Mentorship/coaching:** You've grown engineers to L7; you actively build the technical leadership bench for your domain and can describe the pipeline explicitly.
- **Impact:** You own the multi-year technical roadmap for your domain and hold it through org changes and shifting priorities; your work outlasts any particular team or product cycle — you've changed what "good architecture" means at your company.
- **Communication:** You represent the technical state of your domain to the executive team; your writing and speaking shape how the company thinks about technical quality.

## L9 — Technical leader across the organization, influencing the organizational architecture

Your scope is the organization. You see where the structure of teams and systems creates constraints on what the company can build — and you're making the case to fix it.

- **Engineering competency:** Your judgment on technical debt informs how the company allocates engineering investment; you see where org structure and system architecture are misaligned and make the case to resolve it.
- **Influence:** Your input changes how engineering teams are structured and resourced; executives adjust decisions based on your technical judgment.
- **Mentorship/coaching:** You're developing L7–L8 engineers; your investment in technical leadership has compounding returns across the org.
- **Impact:** You own the 2–3 year technical strategy for a significant portion of the company connected to its competitive position; your decisions have multi-year consequences — the cost of being wrong is measured in years and headcount, not sprints.
- **Communication:** You represent technical constraints and opportunities at the executive level; your communication shapes how leadership allocates capital.

## L10 — Technical leader across the organization, amplifying the organizational architecture

You create the conditions under which great engineering is possible across the company. Your work is systemic and durable.

- **Engineering competency:** You've built the hiring bars, culture, and practices that make strong engineering reproducible and not dependent on any individual; you're accountable for the overall technical health of the company.
- **Influence:** You define what engineering excellence means at this company; your presence shapes technical culture even when you're not in the room.
- **Mentorship/coaching:** You're developing the next generation of L8–L9 technical leaders; the company's technical leadership pipeline is your explicit responsibility.
- **Impact:** You set the technical strategy that anchors the company's multi-year bets; you're accountable for the technical debt that limits what the company can execute on.
- **Communication:** You represent the company's technical credibility externally — to customers, investors, and talent; your writing and speaking shape the company's technical identity in the market.

## On Feedback

Written feedback is the mechanism that makes this framework real. Your manager should help you collect and reflect on feedback — but the documentation is what creates accountability and enables iteration. Verbal feedback evaporates. Written feedback compounds.
